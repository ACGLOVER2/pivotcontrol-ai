# Beta Learnings — Key Findings from the First Audit Cycle

April 2026. Three audits. Three sellers. Three call types. Here is what the first beta cycle revealed about the product, the framework, and the market.

---

## Finding 1: Context Is the Product

The most important insight from beta did not come from the audits themselves. It came from the feedback after the first Antoine Wade audit.

The audit scored 54/100 against a discovery call rubric. Antoine's feedback:

> *"This raises a broader point about how any AI-based call coaching tool needs to operate. Grading a call without account context, prior conversation history, or the defined objective for that specific call will produce findings that are technically observed but strategically incorrect. The tool needs that context before it scores anything."*

Three of five findings were removed when the correct context was applied. The score adjusted to 72/100 and ultimately 88/100 once the full call was reviewed.

**The product implication:** The intake form is not a nice-to-have feature. It is the product. Without it, the audit is a transcript summary with a number attached. With it, the audit is an accurate coaching instrument.

**What changed immediately:**
- Mandatory intake form before any transcript is scored
- CONFIRMED / NOT APPLICABLE finding labels introduced
- Role-specific rubrics formalized (AD on SC-led call vs AD-led call)
- Account intelligence tier added to unlock specific language coaching

---

## Finding 2: Role-Specific Rubrics Are Non-Negotiable

The beta cycle covered three distinct roles on the same account (GDIT):
- Antoine Wade — Account Director (SC-led demo)
- Tom Katayama — Solutions Consultant (same call)
- Kyle Walsh — Solutions Consultant (prior calibration call)

Each role requires a completely different rubric.

The AD's appropriate silence during the SC's technical presentation is correct behavior — not an authority gap. The SC's verbalized confusion is the primary failure mode for that role — not a minor coaching note. These distinctions cannot be made without role context.

**The product implication:** Every intake form must identify the seller's role and their specific function on that call. The audit system must apply role-specific criteria, not a universal communication standard.

---

## Finding 3: The Close Is Always the AD's Responsibility

Across every AD audit in the beta cycle, close ownership was either confirmed as a gap or confirmed as a strength. It was never neutral.

Antoine Wade's first partial audit (POD00002 only) had the close marked as pending — the transcript ended mid-demo. When the second segment (POD00003) was reviewed, the close was fully confirmed: three named next steps, owner-attributed, Todd confirmed.

The rule that emerged is universal:

> *The Account Director always owns the close. Regardless of who drove the call, what the call type was, or how the SC performed — the AD is responsible for the summary, the commitment, the timeline, and the next step.*

This finding held across every call type in the beta cycle. It is now a permanent principle in the role rubric.

## Finding 4: Generic Language Coaching Has Limited Value

The first draft of language reframes in the Antoine Wade audit used account-general bridge lines:

> *"Let me add some context here that might help, Kyle..."*

After Antoine's feedback about account intelligence, the same moment was rewritten with account-specific context:

> *"Kyle — Martin flagged last week that the inspection team is under pressure on cycle time. That is the throughput problem we are mapping. Junior, is the document correlation step the primary bottleneck or is there something upstream?"*

The difference is the account intelligence in the intake: Martin's prior conversation, Junior's role as the technical subject matter expert, the cycle time problem as the business priority.

**The product implication:** The account intelligence section of the intake form is what converts the audit from a useful framework exercise into a specific, deployable coaching instrument. The more account history goes in, the more precise the language coaching becomes. This is the core differentiator from generic communication tools.

## Finding 5: The SC's Primary Failure Mode Is Externalizing Confusion

Kyle Walsh's audit (47/100 — Developing) surfaced the most common SC authority erosion pattern in the beta cycle:

> *"I am struggling here, where we are going to be able to assist. I am still getting a little bit lost."*

Spoken directly to the client during a live discovery call.

This is not a confidence problem. It is a structural problem. When the fit is unclear, the SC's instinct is to say so aloud rather than convert the confusion into a diagnostic question. The correct behavior:

> *"Before I map our solution to this, let me make sure I have the right picture. Walk me through what the inspector sees on screen the moment the document arrives."*

Kyle's best moment on the same call was his platform value summary at 20:25 — five steps, one sentence, no hedging. The gap between those two moments is the entire development opportunity: when Kyle knows the answer, he is authoritative. When the path is unclear, his structure collapses outward.

**The product implication:** SC coaching must address the ambiguity response pattern specifically — not filler words, not pacing. The question to ask is: what does this person do when they don't know the answer yet?

## Finding 6: Founder-Level Positioning Is a Scoreable Skill

Kamau Miller's audit (84/100 — Executive) introduced a finding that doesn't appear in the AD or SC rubrics:

The decision to frame ForgeAI as a company with a mission — not a personal service or a freelance offering — shaped the entire engagement dynamic in a room of established entrepreneurs.

> *"Forge AI Solutions is a company dedicated to providing automated AI-powered tools and systems to help businesses improve efficiency and increase revenue."*

In a room of co-founders, this positioned Kamau as a peer at the table rather than a vendor pitching services. That positioning decision is made in the first sentence and holds or collapses throughout the call.

**The product implication:** The Founder/AE rubric needs a specific criterion for peer positioning — how quickly and firmly founder-level authority is established, and whether it holds under direct comparison questions (ChatGPT vs what you do).

---

## What the Beta Cycle Proved

1. The PCI framework is structurally sound — it surfaces the right patterns across different roles and call types
2. The intake form is essential — without it, the framework produces wrong findings confidently
3. The language coaching is the product's highest-value output — but only when it is account-specific
4. The market exists — three sellers in three different roles all recognized the problem immediately and engaged substantively with the findings
5. The close gap is universal — every seller in the beta cycle had something to learn about close ownership

---

## What Comes Next

**Account Intelligence layer:** Build the intake form into the standard workflow so that every audit automatically has the account context required for specific language coaching.

**SC-specific rubric refinement:** The diagnostic pivot training — converting confusion into a question — is the highest-leverage SC development area and needs dedicated curriculum.

**Founder/AE peer positioning framework:** The Kamau Miller audit revealed a positioning skill that isn't fully captured in the current rubric. It needs its own criteria.

**Repeatability testing:** Run the same framework on three more audits with fresh sellers to test whether the intake-first process consistently produces accurate findings without the correction cycle required in the first Antoine Wade audit.
