# Role Rubrics — What Gets Scored and Why

PivotControl AI scores communication against role-specific rubrics, not a universal standard. The same behavior reads differently depending on what the seller was there to do.

---

## Why Role Matters

An Account Director on an SC-led call who goes silent during the SC's technical presentation is not showing an authority gap. They are doing their job.

An Account Director on an AD-led discovery call who goes silent for 20 minutes is a problem.

Same behavior. Different score. Because the role determines what the call demands.

Every audit begins by identifying the seller's role and the call's objective before a single finding is written.

---

## Account Director — SC-Led Call

**Core responsibility on this call type:**  
The AD is the relationship anchor, SC supervisor, and closer. They are not the discovery lead. They are not the technical expert. They hold the frame, protect the SC, and own the result.

| Responsibility | Expected Behavior | What Gets Scored |
|----------------|-------------------|------------------|
| Opening frame | Structured intro, agenda confirmation, context-setting for new stakeholders | Quality of the open and agenda lock |
| SC handoff | Clean setup line that positions the SC and the demo | How the handoff lands |
| SC protection | Re-entry when the SC signals confusion or loses the room | Whether a bridge line is deployed |
| Strategic interjection | 1–2 moments during the demo that anchor back to account context | Precision and timing of re-entries |
| Passive affirmations | Short confirmatory phrases that signal listening but not leadership | Whether affirmations carry executive weight |
| Close ownership | Summary, commitment, timeline, next steps — always the AD's responsibility | Whether the close is structured and confirmed |

**What is NOT scored against the AD on SC-led calls:**
- Discovery quality (if discovery was completed in a prior session)
- Silence during the SC's technical presentation (this is correct behavior)
- Technical accuracy of the demo content

## Account Director — AD-Led Call

**Core responsibility on this call type:**  
The AD drives the entire conversation — discovery, framing, objection handling, and close. All five PCI metrics apply at full weight.

| Responsibility | Expected Behavior |
|----------------|-------------------|
| Opening frame | Sets agenda, confirms objectives, establishes authority |
| Discovery | Asks questions that surface business-level pain, not just technical requirements |
| Strategic framing | Stays at the outcome level throughout — never drifts tactical |
| Objection handling | Absorbs objections without defensiveness, reframes to business impact |
| Close | Confirms next steps with named owners and specific dates |

## Solutions Consultant — Technical Discovery or Demo

**Core responsibility on this call type:**  
The SC is the primary driver of technical content. They must maintain authority while navigating ambiguity — which is the hardest communication challenge in enterprise sales. When the SC knows the answer, they are authoritative. When the path is unclear, the failure mode is externalizing the confusion.

| Responsibility | Expected Behavior | What Gets Scored |
|----------------|-------------------|------------------|
| Discovery clarity | Asking the right question when fit is unclear — not defending fit before it's confirmed | Diagnostic discipline vs premature pitching |
| Structured explanation | Compress-then-expand — one sentence answer, then detail on request | Response length and compression under pressure |
| Authority under ambiguity | Never verbalize confusion to the client — convert it into a diagnostic question | Whether confusion is internal or external |
| Fit framing | A pre-loaded 60-second platform value statement deployed at the right moment | Timing and precision of fit framing |
| Close support | Support the AD's close — provide technical confirmation, never drive next steps past the AD | Whether the SC overreaches on close |

**The SC's primary failure mode:**  
Saying *"I'm struggling here, I'm not sure where we fit"* to the client. This is internal monologue spoken aloud. It transfers cognitive burden to the buyer and erodes the client's confidence in the team. The correct behavior is to convert confusion into a diagnostic question: *"Before I map our solution to this, let me make sure I have the right picture. Walk me through what happens at the moment the document arrives."*

## Enterprise AE — Executive or Commercial Call

**Core responsibility on this call type:**  
The AE is in the room to advance the deal under executive scrutiny. The primary pressure is rapid-fire questioning from C-suite and VP-level buyers who will test authority, pricing defensibility, and ROI framing simultaneously.

| Responsibility | Expected Behavior | What Gets Scored |
|----------------|-------------------|------------------|
| Executive framing | Answer at the business outcome level before adding any detail | Strategic vs tactical ratio |
| Rapid-fire resilience | Maintain structure under simultaneous questioning from multiple executives | Recovery speed and compression under pressure |
| Pricing authority | Defend pricing by tying it to cost of inaction — never apologize for price | Authority signal stability during commercial discussion |
| Narrative control | Reframe competitive comparisons immediately — never argue on the competitor's terms | Speed and precision of reclaim |
| Close precision | Leave every executive call with a specific named commitment — not just positive momentum | Whether a confirmed next step exists |

**The AE's primary pressure point:**  
Rapid-fire executive questioning. When multiple executives press simultaneously — CFO on pricing, CTO on architecture, VP Procurement on timeline — cognitive load spikes and structure collapses. The AE who pauses, frames the question type, and answers in three sentences or fewer is the one who is perceived as executive-ready.

## Founder / Executive-Level Seller

**Core responsibility on this call type:**  
The Founder is not selling a product — they are establishing peer credibility with buyers who are evaluating whether this person and company are worth their time. The communication challenge is positioning as a founder-peer, not a vendor, while simultaneously demonstrating specific capability through live proof.

| Responsibility | Expected Behavior | What Gets Scored |
|----------------|-------------------|------------------|
| Peer positioning | Frame the company as a peer entity — not a freelancer or vendor — from the first sentence | How quickly and firmly founder-level positioning is established |
| Live proof | Lead with deployed, running systems — not mockups or theoretical capabilities | Whether proof is specific and concrete |
| Differentiation framing | Kill competitor comparisons quickly with a specific proof point, not a feature comparison | Speed of reclaim when general-purpose tools are mentioned |
| Social cue detection | Catch time constraints, attention shifts, and skepticism signals early | Whether pivots happen before or after the signal |
| Process discipline | Refuse to over-promise — anchor to requirements before scoping | Whether commitment discipline holds under pressure to close fast |

## The Universal Rules

Regardless of role or call type, three things are always scored:

1. **Close ownership** — The senior seller always owns the close. The SC never drives next steps past the AD. The AD never lets the SC close a strategic account call.

2. **Authority signal stability** — Tone, pacing, and language confidence should hold across the full call arc, not just at the start and end.

3. **Narrative reclaim** — Every seller should have a pre-loaded reclaim phrase for the moment the conversation drifts to a competitor, a generic tool comparison, or a frame that works against their value proposition.
